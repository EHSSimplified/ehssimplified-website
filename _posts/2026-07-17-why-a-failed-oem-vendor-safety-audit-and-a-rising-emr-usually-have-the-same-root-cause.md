**A failed OEM vendor safety audit and a rising EMR usually come from the exact same gaps on your floor.** OEMs and larger manufacturers now score their suppliers on safety the same way they score them on quality and delivery — and the missing LOTO procedures or unguarded machines that fail an audit are frequently the same conditions driving the claims that set your Experience Modification Rate (EMR — the number your insurance carrier uses to set your premium, where 1.0 is average and anything above it means you're paying more than your peers).

## Why Are OEMs Suddenly Auditing Their Suppliers' Safety Programs?

If you've supplied parts or contract manufacturing to a larger OEM and been asked to complete a safety scorecard, host a plant walkthrough, or register on a platform like Avetta or ISNetworld before a PO gets issued — you're seeing supply chain risk management in action.

OEMs don't want a line stoppage, a recordable injury, or an OSHA citation at a supplier's plant disrupting their production schedule or exposing them to liability. So they push the verification work upstream, onto you, before the contract is signed.

This has moved from "occasional ask" to standard procurement policy across automotive, aerospace, and industrial supply chains over the past several years — and it's not slowing down.

## What Does a Failed Audit Actually Cost a Plant?

This isn't a paperwork inconvenience — it hits the P&L directly:

* **Lost or delayed contracts.** No passing scorecard, no PO — regardless of your price or your capacity.
* **Weeks in corrective-action limbo.** Production schedules move on without you while your file sits incomplete.
* **A rising EMR right alongside it.** Nobody's actively managing the injury data that actually sets your EMR — claims, not audit scores — so it drifts upward at the same time your audit file falls behind, because both trace back to the same unmanaged floor conditions.

That last point compounds. A plant with an EMR of 0.75 and one with an EMR of 1.25 aren't paying similar premiums for similar headcounts — they're often thousands of dollars apart every year, on every line, whether or not an OEM ever asked to see the number.

## The Two Standards Behind Most Manufacturing EMR Problems

Auditors, OSHA inspectors, and your insurance carrier are all effectively looking at the same floor conditions. Two standards drive most of the trouble:

**Lockout/Tagout (29 CFR 1910.147).** Missing or unfollowed energy-control procedures are one of OSHA's most consistently cited standards in manufacturing, and LOTO incidents tend to be severe — which means they're both a fast audit fail and a claim serious enough to move your EMR.

**Machine Guarding (29 CFR 1910.212).** Unguarded point-of-operation hazards are the other perennial citation. Auditors walk the floor specifically looking for this because it's fast to spot — and it's also a leading cause of the injuries that show up on your OSHA 300 log.

If your written program doesn't clearly address both, it's usually the first thing a vendor audit flags — and the first place a claim comes from.

## What Are OEM Auditors and Scorecards Actually Checking?

Uploading a generic safety manual template doesn't work — auditors see through it fast. What actually moves your score:

**A written program that matches your actual lines.** Your specific machines, your specific lockout points, your specific job sites — not a boilerplate document.

**Current, documented training.** Not "we trained everyone in 2022." Ongoing, dated, and tied to specific operators and specific equipment.

**A clean, explainable OSHA 300 log and EMR.** If the numbers aren't great yet, the fix isn't hiding them — it's showing a documented downward trend and a real corrective action plan.

**Proof the program gets used on the floor.** LOTO audits, guarding inspections, incident follow-ups — evidence the program isn't just a binder for show.

**Fast answers when an auditor follows up.** Most audit delays are self-inflicted: nobody internally owns keeping the file current.

## Why This Keeps Happening to the Same Plants

Most small and mid-size manufacturers treat vendor audits as a one-time scramble instead of a standing part of how the plant runs. Someone builds the binder, uploads it, and it sits untouched — by the next audit, half of it is outdated and half your new hires never saw it.

That's the exact gap between plant owners who *think* they're covered and owners who actually are. And it's the same gap that shows up on your OSHA 300 log when your workers' comp renewal notice arrives higher than expected — not because you failed an audit, but because the underlying conditions never got fixed.

## FAQ

**What is an OEM vendor safety audit, and why do manufacturers require it from suppliers?** It's a standardized safety review OEMs use to screen suppliers before issuing purchase orders, so a line stoppage, injury, or citation at a supplier's plant doesn't disrupt their production or expose them to liability.

**Does a failed vendor audit directly affect my EMR?** No — EMR is calculated purely from your actual workers' comp claims history, not from audit scores. But the same gaps that fail an audit (missing LOTO procedures, unguarded machines, undocumented training) are usually the ones generating the claims that raise your EMR, so the two tend to move together.

**What are the most common OSHA citations that hurt a manufacturing EMR?** Lockout/Tagout (29 CFR 1910.147) and Machine Guarding (29 CFR 1910.212) are the two most consistently cited standards in manufacturing, and both tend to produce severe, EMR-moving incidents when missed.

**Do I need a full-time safety manager to pass an OEM vendor audit?** No. Most small and mid-size manufacturers don't need a $5,000/month enterprise platform or a full-time hire — they need a current, line-specific program and someone who owns keeping it that way.

**How often should plant safety documentation be updated for vendor audit purposes?** Continuously, not annually. New operators need training records the day they start, and your OSHA 300 log needs to stay current, not get reconstructed right before an audit.

**What should I do if my EMR is already above 1.0?** Don't hide it. Auditors and carriers respond better to a documented corrective action plan and a visible downward trend than to a number with no explanation attached.
